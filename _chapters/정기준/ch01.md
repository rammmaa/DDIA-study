## 1. Reliability
Reliability is a system's ability to keep its promises, even when things go wrong. 

* **Faults vs. Failures:** A *fault* is when one part of the machine breaks (a disk dies, a bit flips). A *failure* is when the whole system stops serving the user. We want systems that are **fault-tolerant**—where a single fault doesn't cascade into a total failure.
* **Hardware vs. Software:** Hardware used to be the main culprit, but today, software bugs (like a "leap second" crash or a runaway process eating all the RAM) are often more dangerous because they tend to be systematic across all nodes.
* **The Human Factor:** Most outages are caused by human error (bad configs, fat-fingering a command). The best systems assume humans will mess up and provide sandboxes and easy "undo" (rollback) buttons.

## 2. Scalability
Scalability isn't a toggle you turn on; it’s a strategy. It's the answer to: *"If our traffic doubles tomorrow, where does the system snap?"*

### Figuring out the "Load"
You can't scale what you can't describe. We use **load parameters** like:
* **Twitter’s Fan-out:** The classic example. Posting a tweet is easy; delivering it to 50 million followers (the "fan-out") is the hard part. 
* **Response Times & Percentiles:** Don't look at averages—they hide the truth. Look at the **p99 (99th percentile)**. This represents your most valuable (or frustrated) users who are experiencing the "tail latency."

### Vertical vs. Horizontal
* **Scaling Up:** Buying a bigger, beefier server. Simple, but it has a ceiling.
* **Scaling Out:** Throwing a fleet of cheap machines at the problem. It’s more complex to manage but practically limitless.

## 3. Maintainability
Software spends most of its life in "maintenance mode." If a system is a nightmare to change, it’s effectively dead.

1.  **Operability:** Make it easy for the Ops team to see what's happening. If you have good "observability," you can fix things before users even notice.
2.  **Simplicity:** Complexity is the "big ball of mud" that slows everyone down. We fight this with **abstractions**—clean ways to hide the messy details so developers can focus on one piece at a time.
3.  **Evolvability:** Requirements *will* change. A maintainable system is "agile" enough that adding a new feature doesn't require rewriting the entire core.
